{{< quick-answer >}}
Being visibly foreign in Japan means experiencing consistent low-level otherness — stares, people switching to English before you speak, occasional service refusals (onsens with tattoos, some izakayas with "Japanese only" signs). Overt hostility is rare. The more common experience is ambient treatment as an outsider, even after years of residence. It varies significantly by city — Tokyo and Osaka are significantly more foreigner-accustomed than rural areas.
{{< /quick-answer >}}

Nobody puts this in the moving guide. The travel blogs show cherry blossoms and ramen. What they don't prepare you for is the moment you walk into a restaurant and the staff look briefly panicked, or when a shop owner walks toward you with nervous body language, or when someone visibly switches seats on the train.

These experiences are common for visibly non-Japanese people living in Japan. They're not universal, they vary enormously by location and context, and Japan is not an unusually hostile country — but they're real, they accumulate, and not acknowledging them doesn't make them easier to deal with.

---

## What You'll Actually Encounter

### The Stares
In major cities (Tokyo, Osaka, Kyoto), foreigners are common enough that staring is minimal in most contexts. In rural areas, smaller cities, or certain neighborhoods, sustained curious looking is normal. It's rarely hostile — it's often genuine curiosity — but it's persistent and can become tiring.

### The Automatic English Switch
Someone speaks to you in Japanese, you respond in Japanese, they switch to English. This is usually intended as helpful — they're trying to accommodate you. It can still feel dismissive after years of studying Japanese. Responding in Japanese and continuing the conversation in Japanese usually resolves it.

### Service Refusals
**Onsens and public baths:** Many onsen prohibit tattooed customers — including foreigners. Some have explicit "no tattoos" rules; others have "Japanese only" signs that may be enforced inconsistently. Private room onsens (*kazoku-buro*) are almost always accessible regardless. The tattoo-specific rules are gradually relaxing in tourist areas.

**Bars and small izakayas:** A small number of establishments in entertainment districts have operated "Japanese only" or "members only" policies that function as foreigner exclusions. These are not illegal under current Japanese law (there is no comprehensive anti-discrimination statute). They are not representative of Japan broadly, but they exist.

**Apartments:** As covered elsewhere — landlord refusals are common and legal.

### Assumptions About Your Japanese
People will sometimes assume you can't speak Japanese before you demonstrate otherwise. Menus appear in English. Staff speak slowly and loudly. This is usually well-intentioned, but can feel infantilizing after you've been in Japan for years.

---

## What's Rarely Encountered

Overt hostility, verbal aggression, or physical intimidation directed at foreigners is uncommon in Japan by international comparison. Japan is one of the safer countries in the world for foreigners to live in across most dimensions. The discrimination that exists is more often structural and systemic (housing, certain service refusals) than interpersonal aggression.

---

## How Long-Term Residents Navigate This

**On the stares:** Most people stop noticing them after the first year. They become background rather than foreground. Strategies that help: focusing on the specific activity you're doing, rather than ambient social monitoring.

**On automatic English switches:** Reply in Japanese, keep going. Most people follow your lead once you establish it.

**On service refusals:** Know which establishments are foreigner-friendly before you arrive. For onsens: check Japan Onsen Guide or individual onsen websites. For housing: use foreigner-specialist agencies. Having contingencies means refusals are inconvenient, not crushing.

**On accumulation:** The effects of persistent low-level otherness compound over time in ways that are easy to dismiss in any individual instance. Talking about it — in an expat community, with a therapist, with other foreigners who get it — matters more than trying to resolve each incident individually.

---

## The Rising Tension Context (2025–2026)

Japan's foreign population reached nearly 3.95 million in 2025, and the political conversation around immigration has intensified. Some of what circulates on social media — in Japan and internationally — about foreigners in Japan is inflammatory and not representative of most people's actual experience.

The day-to-day reality for most foreign residents remains: most Japanese people are polite, helpful, and genuinely welcoming of individual foreign residents, even when public discourse about immigration is complicated.

Being aware of the broader context is useful. Letting inflammatory social media posts define your daily experience here is not.

---

## Legal Recourse

Japan does not have comprehensive anti-discrimination legislation equivalent to the US, UK, or EU. The Tokyo Metropolitan Government and some other local governments have passed ordinances against "hate speech," but enforcement is limited and housing/service discrimination has no clear civil remedy.

The Japan Federation of Bar Associations (*Nichibenren*) maintains a human rights hotline (0120-007-110, weekdays) that can provide guidance on specific incidents.
